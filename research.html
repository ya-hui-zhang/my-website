Research

Our group works on theoretical condensed matter physics with an emphasis on strongly correlated quantum systems. We develop analytical and numerical approaches to understand exotic quantum phases of matter and quantum phase transitions. Below are our four main research directions.

Moiré Systems

Moiré superlattices—formed by stacking two-dimensional materials with a small twist angle or lattice mismatch—provide a highly tunable platform for engineering nearly flat topological bands where electron–electron interactions dominate. These systems offer unprecedented opportunities to realize and control exotic quantum phases of matter in a tabletop setting.

  • Integer and fractional quantum anomalous Hall phases — A central goal is to understand and predict topological phases that emerge at zero magnetic field in moiré materials and rhombohedral multilayer graphene. Key questions include identifying new fractional quantum anomalous Hall states, understanding quantum anomalous Hall crystals and their interplay with topology, and characterizing the quantum phase transitions between these phases and conventional states.
  • Mott physics in strongly correlated topological bands — When interaction effects are strong enough to localize electrons in topological flat bands, a rich landscape of correlated insulating states emerges, including topological Mott insulators, pseudogap metals, and exotic magnetic orders. We aim to develop a unified theoretical framework for these phenomena, connecting moiré Mott physics to the broader problem of doped Mott insulators in condensed matter.
  • Theory of superconductivity for twisted bilayer graphene — The mechanism of superconductivity in twisted bilayer graphene remains one of the most important open questions in the field. We are developing theories based on strong-coupling approaches—including resonating valence bond states, doped fractional Chern insulators, and t–J model physics—to explain the origin and symmetry of the superconducting order and its connection to the nearby correlated insulating phases.

Cuprate & Nickelate Superconductors

High-temperature superconductivity remains one of the central open problems in condensed matter physics. We develop new theoretical frameworks to understand the cuprate and the recently discovered nickelate superconductors, with the broader goal of uncovering universal principles that govern unconventional superconductivity in strongly correlated materials.

  • Ancilla qubit theory of the pseudogap metal — The pseudogap regime in underdoped cuprates defies description by conventional Fermi liquid theory. We develop a novel theoretical framework using ancilla qubits to construct variational wavefunctions that capture the essential physics of Mott insulators and pseudogap metals. This approach provides a new lens for understanding the transition between the pseudogap state and the Fermi liquid, and connects naturally to topological order and fractionalization.
  • Bilayer nickelate La3Ni2O7 — The discovery of high-temperature superconductivity in pressurized bilayer nickelate La3Ni2O7 opens a new chapter in the study of correlated superconductors. We are developing strong-coupling theories based on multi-orbital t–J models that incorporate Hund’s rule physics, aiming to understand the pairing mechanism, the role of interlayer coupling, and the connection between nickelates and cuprates.
  • Non-Fermi liquid criticality with Fermi surface volume jump — A key feature of strongly correlated metals is the possibility of quantum phase transitions accompanied by a discontinuous change in the Fermi surface volume. We investigate the nature of such deconfined critical points, the emergence of non-Fermi liquid behavior and ghost Fermi surfaces, and the potential for novel superconducting instabilities driven by quantum criticality.

Fractional Phases

Fractionalization—where the collective behavior of many electrons gives rise to emergent quasiparticles carrying fractional quantum numbers—is one of the most profound phenomena in quantum many-body physics. We explore how fractionalized phases emerge, how they can be detected, and what new states of matter arise when they are tuned or perturbed.

  • Quantum spin liquids — Quantum spin liquids are exotic magnetic states that evade conventional symmetry-breaking order and instead support fractionalized spinon excitations and emergent gauge fields. We investigate the stability, classification, and experimental signatures of spin liquids in frustrated magnets and moiré systems, as well as the unconventional superconductors and metals that emerge upon doping these phases.
  • Quantum Hall multilayers — Stacking quantum Hall layers opens a route to engineering new topological phases through interlayer coherence and exciton condensation. We study fractional exciton condensates, anyon superfluids, and the interplay between quantum Hall physics and Hubbard model physics in multilayer moiré systems, aiming to bridge two foundational paradigms in condensed matter theory.
  • Deconfined quantum phase transitions — Conventional Landau theory fails to describe certain quantum phase transitions where fractionalized degrees of freedom emerge at the critical point. We explore deconfined quantum criticalities, dualities between seemingly distinct topological phases, and the broader consequences of these ideas for understanding quantum matter beyond the Landau paradigm.

Numerical Methods

In addition to analytical approaches, our group actively develops and applies state-of-the-art numerical methods to study strongly correlated quantum systems. By going beyond mean-field descriptions, we aim to obtain controlled and unbiased results for challenging many-body problems where analytical methods alone are insufficient.

  • Density matrix renormalization group (DMRG) — DMRG is one of the most powerful methods for one-dimensional and quasi-one-dimensional quantum systems. We use DMRG to study quantum criticality, exotic superconducting states, and entanglement properties in strongly correlated models, providing benchmarks and insights that guide our analytical theories.
  • Variational Monte Carlo (VMC) — VMC provides a flexible framework for constructing and optimizing physically motivated trial wavefunctions in two dimensions. We develop new classes of variational states—including those based on ancilla qubits and parton constructions—to capture Mott physics, Fermi surface reconstruction, and fractionalization in correlated electron systems.
  • Neural network quantum states — Neural network ansatze offer a promising new paradigm for representing quantum many-body wavefunctions, with the expressive power to capture complex entanglement structures that challenge traditional approaches. We are exploring how these methods can be combined with physical insights to tackle open problems in strongly correlated systems.
